Chapter 1: Collecting Data

    The Structure of Data
  • Statistics is the science of collecting, organising, summarising & analysing data
  • It is said that new amount of information & data doubles per year
  • Analysing data is relevant to almost any career path and is very applicable

  • There are 2 types of statistic methods:
      • Descriptive
    • Focuses on collecting, organising & summarising data
      Inferential
    • Focuses on making conclusions about a population based on the sample drawn
    Cases & Variables
  • A variable is a characteristic that is specific to a person or object
  • Variables can be either categorial/qualitative or numerical/quantitative
  • They usually correspond to the number of columns in a dataset, unlike cases which correspond to the number of relevant rows

  • The first column or row that has the names of the variables is the identifier column/row

  • Categorial/Qualitative Variable
      • A non-numerical name for a characteristic/group
      No algebraic or mathematical computations can be performed on these variables
      Examples include blood group, street address, gender

  • Numerical/Quantitative Variable
      • A dimension for measuring the amount of something
      Algebraic/mathematical computations can be performed on these variables
      Can be either discrete or continuous
    • Discrete variables are countable and can only take specific intervals; like number of cars or shoe size
    • Continuous variables vary over time and can include any decimal within an interval; like waiting time
      Examples include time, weight, height, distance

  • Explanatory Variables
      • Variables that explain other the existence or change for response variables

  • Response Variables
      • Variables that respond or react to explanatory variables
    Collecting Data
  • A population is the set of all units/elements in a study. Denoted by "N"
      • A sample is a subset of the population. Denoted by "n"
    • Random samples are best for collecting data and/or conducting experiments
    • Destructive testing on a sample damages or wastes the product and/or sample. This includes car crash testing or tasting food. Dummies or animals or usually used for destructive testing

  • Statistical inference is the process of using data collected from a sample to make inferences about the entire population

  • Sometimes the information gathered from a sample is not faithful. This occurs when sampling bias is used (Selecting a sample to the advantage of the researcher). Volunteers may also be considered sampling bias. Bias, in general, may inaccurately represent a population

  • Methods of Sampling
      • Simple Random Sample (SRS)
    • Randomly selected samples containing equal amount of participants
      Systematic Sampling
    • An interval amount is chosen as "k". A sample is chosen for every k-th interval in the population
      Stratified Sampling
    • Data is divided into strata/groups that share common traits (homogenous)
      Cluster Sampling
    • Data is divided into random clusters/groups, which are then divided further for sampling (heterogenous)
    Types of Studies
  • Association demonstrates a correlation between 2 variables
  • Causation implies one variable directly affects the value of another variable

  • Confounding factors/variables is an external variable that affects the relationship between 2 associated variables

  • An experiment is a study where explanatory variables are controlled by the researcher
      • A randomised experiment is an experiment where the values of the explanatory variables are randomly chosen. Choosing these variables randomly is essential to concluding a causal relationship. Generally, the more random an experiment, the more faithful the results. Types of randomised experiments include:
    • Randomised Comparative Experiment
        • Cases are randomly assigned only one out of more treatments. The results of the response variables for each case is then compared
    • Matched Pairs Experiment
        • Cases receive all treatments in a random order. The response variables for individual cases are then compared

  • An observational study is a study where no variables are controlled by the researcher, rather, are observed in their natural form

  • A control group is the group of participants in a sample that did not receive a treatment. They should not affect the outcome of the response variable

  • The placebo effect is the phenomenom where a person receiving a placebo or fake/dummy treatment obtains the predicted response in an experiment

  • Blinding is a tactic that can be used to strength the integrity of experiments:
      • In a single-blind experiment, only researchers know which group is receiving their treatment; participants do not know

      In a double-blind experiment, neither the researchers nor participants have knowledge of whom receives the respective treatment
