Lecture 3.1: Determinants

    Determinants
  • Determinants (cross products) are used to solve for the area of a parallelogram made by enclosing 2 vectors with each other. The result of determinants is always a scalar. The area is found by combining those 2 vectors into a matrix, with size 2 ⨯ 2, and finding the determinant of the resulting matrix.
      • The resulting matrix must be a square matrix; if not, then the determinant is not valid
  • Given the 2 vectors u & v, the determinant of matrix A is the product of the entries in the diagonal subtracted by the product of the entries in the off-diagonal:
      • determinant(A) = |product(diagonal) - product(off-diagonal)|
      determinant(A) = |u ⨯ v|

  • Changing the order of the vectors in the matrix gives the opposite of the determinant; hence why the absolute value is needed for the true area

  • If one vector is within the span of the other (lie on the same axis; dependent), the determinant of the vectors is always zero

  • Enclosing together 3 vectors in 3D gives the shape of a parallelopiped. The volume of this shape is the determinant of the matrix of the 3 vectors. In this case, the dot product of 2 vectors and the cross product of the resulting vector with the other vector (triple scalar product) gives the volume of the parallelopiped
      • If all 3 vectors are dependent to each other, the determinant is zero

  • Co-factor Expansion is the method used to compute the determinant of a matrix of size n ⨯ n
      • The size of the matrix is reduced by 1 column & row (n - 1) until it is size 2 ⨯ 2:
    • while(size(A) != (2 ⨯ 2)): rows(A) -= 1; columns(A) -= 1;
      Any row & column can be used for the expansion method, but a column & row with more zeros is preferred to simplify arithmatic
      If a row is chosen, the 2 ⨯ 2 matrix that is not part of that row and column is isolated and the determinant of the matrix is calculated. The resulting determinant scalar is then multiplied by its co-factor. The co-factor is the product of the current entry and its corresponding signature, which is (-1)p + q where p = row of entry & q = column of entry. This is continued until there are no more entries left in the chosen row. The determinant of the original matrix is the sum of all 2 ⨯ 2 determinants multiplied by their corresponding signatures

  • If the determinant is zero, the vectors are dependent; if it is not zero, the vectors are independent
    Example 1:
      Find the area S made by enclosing vector u = [[4] [1]] with vector v = [[2] [6]].

    1. Combining the vectors to make matrix A:
        2. A = [[4] [1]] & [[2] [6]]
        A = [[4 2] [1 6]]

    2. Finding the determinant of A (which is equal to the area S):
        3. determinant(A) = (4 * 6) - (2 * 1)
        determinant(A) = 24 - 2
        determinant(A) = 22
    Example 2:
  • Find the determinant of matrix A = [[2 -1 3] [1 4 -2] [-1 0 1]]

      • Choosing the first row to traverse:
    • determinant(A) = 2((4 * 1) - (-2 * 0))(-1)2 + (-1)((1 * 1) - (-2 * -1))(-1)3 + 3((1 * 0) - (4 * -1))(-1)4
    • determinant(A) = 2(4) + (1 - 2) + 3(4)
    • determinant(A) = 8 - 1 + 12
    • determinant(A) = 19
    Example 3:
  • Determine the determinant of matrix B = [[1 2 1 -1] [0 -1 0 1] [4 -3 2 -1] [0 3 0 -2]]

      • Choosing the second row:
    • determinant(B) = 0 (-1) * determinant([[2 1 -1] [-3 2 -1] [3 0 -1]]) - (1) * determinant([[1 1 -1] [4 2 -1] [0 0 -2]]) + 0 (-1) * determinant([[1 2 -1] [4 -3 -1] [0 3 -2]]) + (1) * determinant([[1 2 1] [4 -3 2] [0 3 0]])
    • determinant(B) = - (-2)(1)(2 - 4) + (3)(-1)(2 - 4)
    • determinant(B) = -4 + 6
    • determinant(B) = 2
