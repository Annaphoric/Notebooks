Lecture 6.1: Orthogonality & The Dot Product

    The Dot Product
  • let vector u = [[a1, a2...an]], v = [[b1, b2...bn]] for all real numbers in Rn; The dot product or scalar product is the sum of the products of both respective components for each row:
      • u ⋅ v = ∑k = 1n(ak * bk)

  • The dot product can be used to solve for the angle made between 2 vectors. A negative value for the dot product indicates that the 2 vectors form an angle that is greater than π/2 whilst a positive value indicates that their angle is less than π/2

  • The magnitude or length of a vector u (||u||) is the square root sum of all components within that vector. The magnitude of the vector is also the square root of the dot product of itself:
      • ||u|| = sqrt(∑k = 1n(ak2))
      ||u|| = sqrt(u ⋅ u)

  • If the dot product of a vector with itself is zero or the magnitude is zero (scalar), then the vector must be the zero vector:
      • if(u ⋅ u || ||u|| == 0) {u = 0}

  • The dot product is cumulative: the order is irrelevant

  • The magnitude of a scaled vector is equal to the absolute value of the scalar multiplied by the magnitude of the original vector:
      • ||cu|| = |c| ||u||

  • The dot product of a vector u && the sum of 2 vectors u & w is the sum of the dot product of u with each vector individually:
      • u ⋅ (v + w) = (u ⋅ v) + (u ⋅ w)

  • The dot product of a scaled vector with another vector is the same as the dot product of the 2 original vectors multipliied by the scalar:
      • (cu) ⋅ v = c (u ⋅ v)
    Unit vectors
  • A unit vector is a vector with a length/magnitude of one

  • To convert any nonzero vector into a unit vector, in other words determine its direction, the vector is divided by its scalar magnitude:
      • dir(u) = u / ||u||
    Orthogonality
  • Given nonzero vectors u & v in Rn, u & v are orthogonal/perpendicular only if the dot product of both of them is zero:
      • if(u ⋅ v == 0) {u, v = "orthogonal"}
  • Pythagorean Theorum for Vectors
      • ||u + v||2 = ||u||2 + ||v||2
    Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality Theorum
  • Given any 2 vectors u, v in Rn, the absolute value of the dot product cannot exceed the product of the magnitudes of each vector:
      • |u ⋅ v| ≤ ||u|| ||v||
    Triangle Inequality Theorum
  • Given any 2 vectors u, v in Rn, the magnitude of the sum of the vectors is always less than the sum of each individual vector's magnitude:
      • ||u + v|| ≤ ||u|| + ||v||
  • This is analogous to a triangle where the length of any side is always less than the sum of the other 2 lengths
    Projections
  • The projection (closest distance) of a vector u onto another vector v is the shadow of u onto v. The vector between the projected vector w & u is (u - w). w is a scaled version of v. The dot product of (u - w) && vis zero:
      • proj(u, v) = w

      w = cv

      (u - w) ⋅ v = 0
      (u - cv) ⋅ v = 0
      c = (u ⋅ v) / ||v||2
      w = ((u ⋅ v) / ||v||2) ⋅ v

  • The distance between u & v or u & w is ||u - w||:
      • dist(u, v) = ||v - w||
    Example 1:
  • Given vectoru = [[1] [2] [-3] [1]], w = [[2] [3] [2] [0]], are these vectors orthogonal to each other?

      • Computing the dot product:
    • u ⋅ v = (1 * 2) + (2 * 3) + (-3 * 2) + (1 * 0)
    • u ⋅ v = 2 + 6 - 6 + 0
    • u ⋅ v = 2

    • Since the dot product of the vectors is not zero, the vectors are not orthogonal
