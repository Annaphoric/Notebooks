Lecture 2.12: Gas Exchange

    Introduction
  • In the lungs, O2 travels from the alveoli to the blood and CO2 travels from the blood to the alveoli
  • In tissue, O2 travels from the blood to the tissue and CO2 travels from the tissue to the blood

  • The amount of gas in an area is given by its partial pressure P (out of every other gas in that same region). Pressures always move along a pressure gradient, from higher partial pressure to a lower one. The average partial pressures for various regions are:
      • In the atmosphere, PO2 = 160mmHg & PCO2 = 0.3mmHg
      In the alveoli, PO2 = 105mmHg & PCO2 = 40mmHg
      In veins, PO2 = 40mmHg & PCO2 = 46mmHg
      In arteries, PO2 = 100mmHg & PCO2 = 40mmHg
      In cells, PO2 < 40mmHg & PCO2 < 46mmHg
      In the mitchondria, PO2 < 5mmHg
    Ventilation
  • Changes in gaseous partial pressures & volumes during respiration
  • The average change in volume during ventilation is 4L

  • Hyperventilation
      • Also referred to as over-breathing
      CO2 production is less than alveolar ventilation since breathing is occurring too rapidly and more CO2 is being exhaled
      Air volume increases, PO2 slightly increases and PCO2 decreases

  • Hypoventilation
      • Also referred to as under-breathing
      CO2 production is greater than alveolar ventilation since breathing is occurring too slowly and less CO2 is being exhaled
      Air volume decreases, PO2 decreases and PCO2 increases
    Hemoglobin
  • A protein containing 4 polypeptide subunits normally found in all red blood cells (RBCs)
  • The main purpose of hemoglobin (Hb) is to bind to O2 & deliver it to tissues in the body. One Hb molecule can bind to up to 4 O2 molecules (one for each subunit)
  • Hb can be either completely saturated with O2 or completely lack O2 but rarely partially saturated. Hemoglobin saturated with O2 is referred to as oxyhemoglobin (HbO2) and unusaturated hemoglobin is referred to as deoxyhemoglobin (Hb)

  • Most O2 found in the body is bound to hemoglobin

  • % Hb-saturation is calculated by determining the amount of O2 bound to Hb and dividing it by the maximum capacity of Hb to bind to O2 in the form of a percentage. % Hb-saturation is usually plotted on the vertical axis of the Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve while PO2 in mmHg is plotted horizontally
      • % Hb-saturation = ((Bound O2 to Hb)(100%)) / Hb-Binding-Capacity
      The O2-Hb Dissociation Curve demonstrates that the % of Hb that is saturated with O2 is dependent on PO2. As PO2 increases, the % of HbO2 also increases until PO2 reaches venous partial pressure of O2 (40mmHg). At this point, the slope of the tangent line to the curve decreases (the % plateaus to 100%) and the % of HbO2 is 80%
    • This plateau ensures that tissues obtain the proper amount of O2 even in high altitudes (conditions having a low PO2-atm)
      This curve proves that Hemoglobin molecules can only be completely saturated or unsaturated with O2. If one O2 molecule binds to a heme of Hb, Hb will change shape to have much greater affinity for 3 more O2 molecules (high cooperativity)
    • High cooperativity displays ideal conditions to unload its O2 content to tissues
      Within the difference between venous PO2 (40mmHg) & arterial PO2 (100mmHg), around 20% of HbO2 unloads its O2 content to tissues
    Factors Affecting the Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve
  • A rightwards shift of the curve can indicate an increase in metabolic activity, temperature, PCO2, blood acidity (H+ ion concentration; decreased pH; due to an increae in PCO2), 2,3-diphosphglycerate (DPG; BPG; produced during glycolysis and has an affinity for Hb but is reversible)
      • All of these result in a lower affinity of Hb to bind to O2 and more HbO2 unloading. There would be less % of HbO2 at a given PO2

  • A leftwards shift of the curve is causes and results in inverse effects as in a rightwards shift

  • Fetal Hb has a higher affinity for O2 than adult Hb, causing less unloading of O2. PO2 of fetal Hb can only reach a max of 35mmHg max

  • Anemia is a condition characterised by fewer normal RBCs than a normal amount
      • With fewer RBCs, there are fewer total hemoglobin molecules in the body
      In terms of the O2-Hb Dissociation Curve, the shape of the curve remains almost the same as in a normal curve (same high level of cooperativity), but there is a lower overall magnitude of HbO2 in the body

  • Carbon monoxide (CO) is a toxin that has a 200 times more affinity for heme than O2
      • At 50% CO-Hb, the potential of delivering O2 to tissues is halved
      In terms of the curve, the shape of curve changes and there is a lower level of cooperativity
    Distribution of CO2 in the Body
  • Average Distribution of CO2:
      • 10% is dissolved in blood as a free molecule
      25-30% is bound to Hb as a carbamide group, forming carbamino-Hb
      60-65% is converted to HCO3- (bicarbonate ion)

  • Carbonic anhydrase is the enzyme that catalyses the synthesis reaction of H2CO3 (Carbonic acid) from H2O & CO2
      • H2O + CO2 ➝ H2CO3 (catalysed by carbonic anhydrase)
  • H2CO3 is then sponaneously decomposed into HCO3- & H+ ions
      • H2CO3 ➝ HCO3- + H+
      Therefore, increasing [CO2] decreases pH
