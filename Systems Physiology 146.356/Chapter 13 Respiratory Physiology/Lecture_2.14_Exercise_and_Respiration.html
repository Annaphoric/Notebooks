Lecture 2.14: Exercise & Respiration

    Minute Ventilation
  • Measured as the average amount of air volume that flows in & out of the body per minute (units in L/min)
  • Minute ventilation is around 9L/min at normal resting level

  • Not affected much at an arterial PO2 of around 60mmHg or higher, but is affected more at a partial pressure lower than 60mmHg. If PO2 is lower than 60mmHg due to lung disease or high altitude, minute ventilation increases
      • Peripheral chemoreceptors detect low levels PO2 and act to adjust it by increasing ventilation
      Since mild to moderate anemia does not affect PO2 much, minute ventilation does not change if it is present

  • Contrarily, any change of arterial PCO2, regardless of how much, will alter minute ventilation
      • Both peripheral & central chemoreceptors are able to detect changes in arterial PCO2 or [H+]. Central chemoreceptors are most likely to be stimulated by these changes
      Even metabolic H+ ions (not relating to levels of CO2; like in lactic acid production) itself can stimulate chemoreceptors, specifically peripheral chemoreceptors which are most sensitive to these changes
    Changes in Gas Concentrations during Exercise
  • Arterial PO2 usually remains constant (100mHg) regardless of the amount of exercise

    • Normally at rest, minute ventilation is around 9L/min, arterial PCO2 is 40mmHg, and arterial [H+] is 36nmol/L
  • During moderate exercise, minute ventilation increases to about 30L/min, arterial PCO2 decreases slightly to around 38mmHg, and arterial [H+] increase slightly to around 38nmol/L

  • During maximal exercise, minute ventilation increases to about 90L/min, arterial PCO2 decreases to around 30mmHg (since exhalation of CO2 occurs faster than production of metabolic CO2), and arterial [H+] increases to around 54nmol/L (due to lactic acid buildup)

  • As mentioned in the previous chapter, the limiting factor of strenuous exercise is cardiac output, rather than ventilation

  • Once an exercise training is initiated, minute ventilation immediately increases. This is because:
      • Reflexes of mechanoreceptors in joints & muscles are stimulated
      Body temperature is increased
      The brain communicates with motor neurons to fire
      Not chemical or gaseous changes
    Respiratory Abnormalities
  • Hypoxia
      • A deficiency in O2 delivery to tissues
      Causes include:
    • Hypoventilation
        • Accompanied by an increase in PCO2

    • Diffusion Impairment
        • Results from the thickening of alveolar membranes and decrease of their surface area

    • Ventilation-perfusion Inequality
        • Observed as the most common reason for hypoxia
        Seen in Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) like emphysema or chronic bronchitis (characterised by airway inflammation & excess mucus production)

  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease
      • Also referred to as COPD
      A type of COPD is Emphysema
    • As mentioned previously, emphysema is characterised by the merging of alveoli
    • Results in a decrease total surface area for gas exchange, increased compliance, decreased elasticity, increased airway resistance and the workload of respiration
        • If breathing requires more work, hypoventilation can occur where PCO2 increases & PO2 decreases (hypoxia)

  • High Altitudes
      • At a high altitude like on Mount Everest, the Patm drops to around 253mmHg compared to sea level where Patm is normally 760mmHg. For this reason, PO2 on Mount Everest is around 53mmHg compared to at sea level where PO2 is normally greater than 100mmHg, even though the percentage of O2 in the air remains at a 21% concentration

      Benefits of high altitude training include:
    • An increase in erythropoeitin (EPO; a hormone produced by the kidneys), leading to an increase in erythrocytes (RBCs), hemoglobin molecules and arterial PO2
        • This effect is similar to the effects caused by blood doping (injection of hematocrit right before an athletic event, giving an athlete an unfair advantage in these competitions)

    • An increase in number & density of skeletal capillaries (angiogenesis), leading to more efficient O2 delivery

    • An increase in the amount of mitochondira, especially in muscle tissue

    • An increase in myoglobin
