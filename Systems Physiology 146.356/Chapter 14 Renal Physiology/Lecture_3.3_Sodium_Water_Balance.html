Lecture 3.3: Balance of Sodium & Water

    Reabsorption within the Proximal Tubule
  • Most of the H2O that gets filtered is also reabsorbed back into the blood

  • On average, about 2.85L of water is excreted from our body everyday via skin + lungs (0.9L), sweat (50mL), feces (100mL) & urine (1.8L). This implies that we would also require an intake of the same amount of water to make up for the daily loss

  • On average, a total amount of 8.5g of sodium is excreted from our body via sweat (250mg), feces (250mg), & urine (8g). This means the same amount is required from food

  • Both Na+ ions & H2O are filtered freely, highly reabsorbable, & not secreted. Much energy is required to reabsorb both of them. About 2/3 of reabsorption of sodium & water occurs in the PCT and the rest is hormonally controlled within the DCT & collecting duct

  • Na+ reabsorption is active (requires energy) and occurs anywhere within the renal tubule except for the descending limb of the Loop of Henle

  • H2O reabsorption is passive (does not require energy; via osmosis) and depends on Na+ reabsorption
    Reabsorption of Sodium within the PCT
  • There is a higher Na+ concentration within the renal tubular lumen (fluid), interstitial fluid & peritubular capillary, but a lower concetration within a proximal tubular epithelial cell

  • The apical membrane of a PCT epithelial cell is connected to the PCT while the basolateral membrane is facing towards the interstitial fluid and pointing towards a peritubular capillary. This gradient arises through the mechanism of Na-K pumps on the basolateral membrane. This is an active process. Sodium-cotransporters (also called symports) located on the apical membrane help transport other substances like glucose against their gradient into the cell as Na+ ions travel along their gradient into the cell. This requires the active transport of sodium/potassium pumps; so, the symporters are considered to work as a secondary transport. Sodium-antitransporters (also called antiports) located on the apical membrane help transport substances like H+ ions against their gradient out of the cell (secretion) as Na+ ions travel along their gradient into the cell

  • Organic molecules like glucose travel from the cell into the interstitial fluid passively via facilitated diffusion. Na+ ions also travel from the cell into the interstitial fluid, but this occurs actively via sodium-potassium pumps. Bulk flow (measured in osmolarity or mOsm) of these substances drives them to enter the peritubular capillary (reabsorption)

  • The amount of Na+ ions excreted is equal to the amount of Na+ filtered subtracted by the amount of Na+ reabsorbed:
      • excreted = filtered - reabsorbed
      Na+ ions do not get secreted!
    Reabsorption of Water within the PCT
  • H2O molecules requires a transporter known as aquaporins to passively cross the membrane (facilitated diffusion). Aquaporins are abundant on the PCT

  • Passive transport of Na+ ions into the cell from the tubular lumen decreases local osmolarity (pressure of substances relative to pressure of H2O). This drives H2O molecules to leave the tubular lumen into the cell via osmosis (water follows the solute) to attempt to achieve an equal osmolarity. H2O will then flow into the interstitial fluid and into the peritubular capillaries via bulk flow. Solutes do not follow water

  • H2O reabsorption occurs independent of Na+ ions in the collecting duct (CD). This occurs via the mechanism of anti-diuretic hormone (ADH; vasopressin). ADH stimulates the insertion of aquaporins into the apical membrane of epithelial CD cells via a signaling cascade
      • There are 6 steps in the mechanism of ADH:
    1. ADH secreted from the posterior pituitary gland binds to a metabotropic receptor on the basolateral membrane of an epithelial CD cell
    2. After a few intermediate steps, Adenylyl cyclase becomes activated, converting ATP into cAMP
    3. cAMP activates protein kinase-A (PKA)
    4. PKA phosphorylates proteins that increase the rate vesicle fusion onto Aquaporin-2 (AQP2) in the apical membrane
    5. H2O is now able to flow through AQP2's from the renal tubular lumen into the epithlial CD cell
    6. H2O can now flow through AQP3 & AQP4 located on the basolateral membrane from the epithelial CD cell into the interstitial fluid and into the pertiubular capillary. These aquaporins permanently remain within the basolateral membrane

  • ADH does not affect movement of solutes

  • Liquor (alcohol) inhibits the release of ADH, causing more H2O to be excreted in the urine. Diabetes insipidus causes also down-regulates the release of ADH; individuals with this condition can excrete 25L of H2O per day
