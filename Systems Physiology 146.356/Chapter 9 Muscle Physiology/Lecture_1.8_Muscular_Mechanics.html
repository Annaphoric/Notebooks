Lecture 1.8: Muscular Mechanics

    Force-Velocity Relationship during Contraction
  • Force & velocity have an inverse relationship. This higher the force, the lower the velocity (Like pushing against a wall, there is no velocity, but force is at its max)
  • As sarcomeres are shortened isometrically, tension is created throughout the muscle providing it with strength, even if no movement by the muscle overall is done
  • The force is dependent is on the amount of actin & myosin cross-bridges; the more cross-bridges, the stronger the force
  • The velocity is the rate it takes for one iteration of the cross-bridge cycle to occur
      • This rate is dependent on the mechanism of myosin-head ATPases
    Force-Frequency Relationship during Contraction
  • A single contraction stimulus of the muscle is known as a twitch
  • Repeating contraction stimuli where the tension sums up without the muscles relaxing is unfused tetanus
      • This usually occurs when a muscle is stimulated to contract around every 100ms
      Even higher frequency stimuli causes fused tetanus
    • In this case, Ca2+ ATPases cannot keep up with the release of Ca2+ ions from the SR (sarcoplasmic reticulum) to the sarcoplasm (Calcium transient time)
    Force-Length Relationship during Contraction
  • This relationship varies on the length and is optimal at a medial length
  • The greatest amount of tension is produced where there is the appropriate contraction length of the sarcomere
      • This occurs when there is an optimal level actin-myosin interactions
      At this length, the muscle develops the greatest isometric active tension
    • This tension is made by cross-bridge linkages and occurs when delivering a force

  • Titin is the protein responsible for passive tension (resting tension) in muscle
      • This tension is not made via cross-bridge linkages and occurs during stretching exercises
