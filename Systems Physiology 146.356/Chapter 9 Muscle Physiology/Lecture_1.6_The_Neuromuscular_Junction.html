Lecture 1.6: The Neuromuscular Junction

    Muscle Tissue
  • Muscle is one of the 4 types of tissue (Epithelial, Connective & Nervous are the others)
  • Muscle cells are able to store Ca2+ ions within its sarcoplasmic reticulum, even when extracellular Ca2+ levels are normally higher outside the cell
  • Muscle Anatomy
      • Muscle tissue is arranged within tendons that work alongside connective tissue. Skeletal muscle cells are long muscle fibers containing many myofibrils. Myofibrils are made up of many sarcomeres which are the main functional units of a skeletal muscle cell. Sarcomeres have multiple defined lines that show where they begin, end and their relevant points. Every sarcomere is bordered between 2 Z lines and has an M-line in the centre. The sarcomere also labels the different regions known as the H-zone, I-band & A-band depending on if actin and/or myosin filaments are present. The H-zone tells the location where actin filaments are not present, only myosin filaments. The I-band tells the location where myosin filaments are not present, only actin filaments. The A-band tells the location where both actin & myosin filaments are present. The M-line is within the H-zone & A-band and the Z-line is within the I-band
    Muscle Relaxation/Contraction
  • There are 4 main proteins, ATP and a few ions that are involved in muscle contraction
  • The 2 contractile proteins are:
      • Actin
    • A type of microfilament also known as a thin filament
      Myosin
    • A type of filament also known as a thick filament
    • Contains light chains near the head and heavy chains as the tail
    • Acts as a receptor that binds to ATP
  • The 2 Regulatory Proteins are:
      • Troponin
    • A globular protein always bound to actin & tropomyosin
    • Acts as a receptor that binds to Ca2+ ions
      Tropomyosin
    • A type of filament always bound to troponin & actin

  • During relaxation:
      • Ca2+ ions are not present near the filaments
      Troponin does not move tropomyosin off of the myosin-binding sites on actin (thin filament regulation)
      Actin cannot bind to myosin heads/beads
      ATP is bound to myosin heads, separating them from actin

  • During contraction:
      • Ca2+ ions are bound to troponin
      Troponin moves tropomyosin off of the myosin-binding sites of actin (thin filament regulation)
      Actin binds myosin heads/beads, forming a cross-bridge linkage
      ADP + Pi or nothing is bound to myosin heads, causing contraction
    Muscle Transduction during Contraction
  • There are 3 major steps that are part of the transduction for muscle movement:
      • Excitation
        There are 5 steps that occur for/during excitation:
      1. An AP propagates down an α-motor neuron. This is the neuron that synapses with a muscle cell
      2. Voltage gated Ca2+ channels located on the pre-synaptic terminal of the α-motor neuron open
      3. Ca2+ ions enter the α-motor neuron and the excitatory neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh) is released into the neuromuscular junction (synaptic cleft of neuron & muscle cell) from vesicles within the pre-synaptic terminal
      4. 2 Acetylcholine molecules bind to ionotropic nicotinic receptors on the end plate (muscle cell membrane involved in neuromuscular synapse), allowing Na+ ions to enter the muscle cell and K+ ions to leave the muscle cell. Na+ ions entering the cell have a greater effect than K+ ions leaving the cell, causing end plate to become slightly depolarised
      5. An end plate potential (a type of local potential) occurs
