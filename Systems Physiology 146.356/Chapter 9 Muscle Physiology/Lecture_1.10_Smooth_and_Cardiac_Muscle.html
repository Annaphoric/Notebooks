Lecture 1.10: Smooth & Cardiac Muscle

    Smooth Muscle Features
  • Unlike skeletal & cardiac muscle, smooth muscle does not have striations (due to presence of A-bands & I-bands)
      • As a result, smooth muscle tends to be less ordered in shape & size
  • Individual smooth muscle cells are smaller than skeletal muscle cells
  • Smooth muscle cells only have 1 nucleus per cell unlike the other muscle cell types
  • Smooth muscle cells are able to mitotically divide for hyperplastic growth
  • These cells do not have troponin but interestingly, have tropomyosin for an unknown reason
  • These types of cells are thick-filament regulated and not thin-filament regulated like in other muscle cell types
      • Myosin heads change shape instead of actin
  • Myosin ATPases have a low [rate of activity]/[velocity]
  • Ca2+ ions can come from both/either the extracellular environment & the sarcoplasmic reticulum
      • Ca2+ ions are removed from the cytosol via active transport using Ca2+ ATPases and enter the SR or extracellular space
  • Smooth muscle cells do not have T-tubules
  • Action potentials in these cells are much longer, slower & variable
  • The membrane potential is depolarised by the influx of extracellular Ca2+ ions, rather than Na+ ions
      • A stimulus can be innervation via a neuromuscular junction. This can occur spontaneously, by the binding of neurotransmitters or hormones (like Nitric oxide with causes smooth muscle relaxation), or by a change in the chemical or physical environment
    Smooth Muscle Contraction
  • This contraction is heavily dependent on [Ca2+]cytosolic
      • There are 5 major steps for/during smooth muscle contraction:
    1. After a stimulus that increases [Ca2+]cytosolic occurs, cytosolic Ca2+ ions bind to and activates calmodulin
    2. The Calcium-calmodulin complex binds to myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK), activating it
    3. The calmodulin-MLCK complex phosphorylates myosin head's light-chain using a Pi group from ATP
    4. The phosphorylated myosin head stretches away from the myosin filament and attaches to actin via a cross-bridge linkage
    5. As long as [Ca2+]cytoslic remains high, myosin heads are able to phosphorylate and continue iterating through the cross-bridge cycle. When a stimulus to decrease [Ca2+]cytosolic occurs, myosin light-chain phosphatases (MLCPs) remove the phosphate group from the myosin heads, drawing them away from actin (relaxation)
    Cardiac Muscle Features
  • Like skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle cells have sarcomeres, striations, troponin + tropomyosin, and T-tubles
  • Like smooth muscle, cardiac muscle cells are small and only have one nucleus per cell
  • Cardiomyocytes (cardiac muscle cells) cannot undergo tetanus because of their L-type Ca2+ channels prolonged current. This allows the duration of their action potentials to be extended via a long refractory period (200-300ms) (compared to skeletal muscle cells which have a refractory period of 1-2ms)
      • This is crucial for survival since the heart must always relax to fill up with blood and pump it
    Cardiac Muscle Contraction
      There are 6 major steps for/during cardiac muscle contraction:
    1. Pacemaker cells located in the SA node induce an action potential in cardiomyocytes, permitting the influx of Na+ via voltage-gated Na+ channels and depolarising the the membrane
    2. L-type Ca2+ channels within the membrane open, allowing the influx of Ca2+ ions into the sarcoplasm
    3. Calcium-induced Ca2+ release (CICR) occurs. Extracellular Ca2+ ions cause an intracellular cascade, opening ryanodine receptors which causes the efflux of Ca2+ ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the sarcoplasm
    4. The elevated [Ca2+]intracellular allows Ca2+ to migrate to the actin & myosin filaments and bind to troponin. This initiates the cross-bridge cycle
    5. Once contraction is complete, Ca2+ ATPases remove intracellular Ca2+ ions by returning the Ca2+ ions back into the SR and/or extracellular space. Na+/Ca2+ exchangers (antiports) also move Ca2+ ions into the extracellular space via the influx of Na+ ions
    6. Like in skeletal muscle, voltage gated K+ channels open to transport K+ out of the cell, causing hyperpolarisation
