Lecture 1.7: Skeletal Muscle Contraction

    EC Coupling
      There are 4 steps that make up this process:
    1. An AP is initiated by the sum of end plate potentials and propagates down the sarcoplasmic membrane (muscle membrane not part of the end plate and contains voltage-gated Na+ channels) and into the T-tubules (invaginations of the sarcoplasmic membrane)
    2. The depolarisation of the T-tubule causes DHP receptors (dihydropyridine receptors) located on the T-tubular membrane to open their attached ryanodine receptor (a voltage-gated Ca2+ efflux channel)
    3. Ca2+ ions exit the sarcoplasmic reticula (smooth endoplasmic reticula of the muscle cell located near the T-tubule) and enter the sarcoplasm (muscle cell cytoplasm)
    4. These Ca2+ ions travel towards the A-band and bind to troponin, preparing the muscle cell for contraction
    [Contraction]/[Cross-Bridge Cycling]/[Sliding Filament Theory]
      There are 4 steps that occur in one iteration of the cycle:
    1. The [myosin-head]-[ADP]-[Pi] energised cross-bridge complex binds to actin
    2. ADP + Pi are released from the myosin head, causing a power stroke (the bending of the myosin-head, eliciting a force)
    3. ATP, if available, binds to the myosin-head, dissociating it from the actin filament (relaxation). If ATP is not available, the myosin-head will remain bent (contracted) and causes a condition known as rigor mortis (the stiffening of skeletal muscles; usually seen a few hours after death)
    4. The myosin-head ATPase domain hydrolyses ATP into ADP + Pi (this is the [rate-determining]/[slow] step) and re-energises the cross-bridge linkage. If Ca2+ is still available, return to step 1
    Muscle relaxation
  • There are several events that occur during muscle relaxation:
      • The AP ceases or does not occur
      Acetylcholine esterase breaks down acetylcholine into an acetic acid & choline
      The flow of Ca2+ ions out of the sarcoplasmic reticulum stops
      Troponin no longer has a bound Ca2+ ion and is deactivated
      Myosin-binding sites on the actin filament are blocked by tropomyosin
      Circa pumps (Ca2+ ATPase pumps) recycle Ca2+ back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum. This step occurs at a slower rate than the release of Ca2+ ions into the sarcoplasm
  • Calcium transient time is the amount of time it takes for Ca2+ ions to move from/to different regions of the muscle cell
    Drugs that Poison Neuromuscular Junction Events
  • Some of these poisons include:
      • Organophosphates
    • A common ingredient in insecticides that blocks acetylcholine esterase, forcing nicotinic receptors to stay open and inactivates voltage-gated Na+ channels. Insects that ingest this poison die by contraction paralysis
      Vecuronium
    • A paralytic agent used during surgery to calm muscles, including the diaphragm. This helps cardiothoracic surgeons have better accuracy since the chest isn't constantly inflating & deflating. The patient must be artificially ventilated to maintain air circulation during the procedure
    • Functions as an acetylcholine antagonist, which prohibits acetylcholine from binding to nicotinic receptors
