Lecture 1.9: Muscle Fatigue & Fiber Types

    Muscle Fatigue
  • A decrease in the force of the muscle due to repeated work
  • There are 2 types:
      • High Intensity Fatigue
    • Occurs after performing subsequent high intensity workouts (HITs)
    • Physiologically, the muscle cannot recycle/remove excess metabolites like H+ (from lactic acid buildup), K+, Mg2+ ions, Pi, ADP and others. They build up in the muscle cell and interfere with Calcium transient time (Ca2+ release, recycling, storage and others) as a result. One way this occurs is by the precipitation of Ca2+ by an inorganic phosphate, decreasing the total amount of free Ca2+ ions. Troponin also becomes desensitised to Ca2+ ions, decreasing max force. The cross-bridge linkage is directly inhibited

      Endurance Fatigue
    • Occurs after performing a workout for an extended period of time (over 2 hours)
    • Physiologically, the muscle lacks available oxidisable substrates such as muscle glycogen or blood glucose (not ATP). Ryanodine receptors become leaky and release excess Ca2+ ions to buildup in the muscle cell. This excess amount of Ca2+ is able to activate proteases that degrade actin & myosin filaments, leading to post-workout soreness and weakness (the day after)
    • Leads to Central Fatigue which is a decrease in amount of nerve impulses (APs)
        • Interestingly, highly motivated athletes do not experience this type of fatigue since their bodies have adjusted to perform consistent workouts/marathons
    Dimensional Relationships during Muscle Fatigue
  • Force-Velocity Relationship
      • A decrease in both the maximum force & velocity occurs during fatigue. Specifically, max force is lessened once precipitation of Ca2+ & Pi occurs and max velocity is lessened when myosin-head ATPases (rate-determining enzymes) become disrupted

  • Force-Calcium Relationship
      • A decrease in the peak force and and calcium sensitivity occurs during fatigue. At a given force, a fatigued muscle fiber requires more Ca2+ than a normal fiber; at a given Ca2+ level, a fatigued fiber delivers less force than a normal fiber

  • A fiber type I & type II cannot be interconverted between one another because of the large difference in myosin ATPase structure. Although, a fiber type II-a & type II-x can, in fact, be interconverted with the other. Fiber type II-a's are converted to fiber type II-x's via frequent power & strength training. Fiber II-x's are converted to fiber type II-a's via frequent endurance training
  • Genetics plays a large role in percentage of fiber type composition
    Dimensional Relationships based of Muscle Fiber Types
  • As long as the fiber diameter remains constant, type I and type II fibers both generally produce the same force for a given [Ca2+]
  • The max velocity of type II-x fibers is higher than that of type I fibers
  • Type II-x fibers respond quicker (higher cooperativity) to an increase in Ca2+ than type I which have a more gradual increase in force relative to the [Ca2+]
    Motor Units
  • A motor unit includes the α-motor neuron and all of the muscle fibers it innervates (synapses with)
      • A single motor unit can only have and innervate one type of muscle fiber
  • Type II-x motor units have a higher total amount of total fibers than type I motor units
      • For this reason, motor unit type II-x's provide much greater tension force than any other motor unit type

  • Recruitment Principle
      • Once a force is initiated, motor units are always recruited in the order: type I, type II-a's, type II-x's
    Effects of Aging on Muscle
  • Research has shown that the muscle's max force declines by 30%-40% between the ages of 30-80
      • Specifically, fast fibers are degraded as age increases
      The degree of this effect can be lessened by performing strength training exercises
