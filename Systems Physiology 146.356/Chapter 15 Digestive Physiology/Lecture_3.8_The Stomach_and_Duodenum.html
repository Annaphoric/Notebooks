Lecture 3.8: The Stomach & Duodenum

    The Stomach
  • The stomach is a storage organ
  • Complete digestion does not occur in the stomach but more partial breakdown of food occurs (like partial digestion of proteins; carbs & lipids are not significantly digested). Although, no absorption occurs in the stomach

  • Cells located within the stomach include:
      • Parietal cells
    • Secretes stomach acid containing HCl
        • Stomach acid kills most bacteria that enters the body via food. It also promotes protein denaturation
    • Secretes intrinsic factor
        • Binds to vit-B12 to absorb it for RBC production
        The only indespensible, necessary part of the stomach. Otherwise, an individual can live without their stomach

      Chief cells
    • Secretes pepsinogen (the precursor to the enzyme pepsin for protein digestion)

      G-cells
    • Secretes gastrin to stimulate parietal cells
    • A type of enteroendocrine cell

  • There are 3 main regions of the stomach:
      • Fundus
    • The protruding, superior-most region
      Body
    • The centre region
      Antrum
    • The inferior region
    HCl Production in the Stomach
  • HCl is secreted by epithelial parietal cells. Their basolateral membrane is closer to blood vessels while the apical membrane is connected to the stomach lumen

  • Parietal cell activity is increased if a meal high is proteins is ingested. H+/K+ ATPases bind to the apical membrane to release H+ ions into the lumen when stimulated. Gastrin, Histamine, & Acetylcholine promote this process whilst Somatostatin demotes it. These hormones travel from the blood into the parietal cell via the basolateral membrane, initiating a secondary messenger cascade
      • It is shown that HCl can inhibit its own production by inhibiting the entry of gastrin (negative feedback mechanism)
      Protein/polypeptide content increases HCl secretion by stimulating the entry of gastrin. They also act as a buffer by binding to H+ ions, slightly increasing stomach pH (ceasation of negative feedback mechanism)
      The H+/K+ pumps act as antiports by transferring H+ ions from the parietal cell into the stomach lumen and K+ ions from the stomach lumen into the parietal cell both against their gradient (active transport)

  • There are also passive K+ leak channels on the apical membrane (transported from the parietal cell into the stomach lumen)
  • The source of H+ and HCO3- ions within the cell is via CO2 as it is converted to carbonic acid via carbonic anhydrase. Cl- ions passively travel through leak channels on the apical membrane from the parietal cell into the stomach lumen. There are Cl-/HCO3- pumps on the basolateral membrane which actively pump Cl- ions from the blood into the parietal cells & passively transfer HCO3- ions from the parietal cell into the blood. Cl- ions pumped into the stomach lumen pair with H+ ions to form HCl
    Protein Digestion
  • Chief cells secrete pepsinogen (a zymogen) which is a precursor to pepsin. Pepesinogen is readiy converted to pepsin at a low pH environment like the stomach, but not at a neutral pH like the small intestine. Pepsinogen secretion occurs hand-in-hand with HCl secretion
      • Pepsin performs 20% of protein digestion. Specifically, the protein will be broken up into smaller peptides. The rest of protein digestion occurs in the small intestine
    Gastric Motility
  • Peristaltic waves drive stomach contraction and the propelling of contents further down the stomach and eventually into the small intestine via the pyloric sphincter
      • The pyloric sphinctor divides the stomach from the duodenum of the small intestine. Its main job is to limit the amount of chyme that enters the small intestine
      Peristalsis also mixes around food with stomach fluid to form chyme

  • The presence of foods high in lipids inhibits gastric emptying, slowing down digestion & drug absorption

  • The stomach can hold around 0.5-4L of food/fluid within it
    The Small Intestine
  • The lining of the small intestine is highly convoluted to increase surface area. This lining is known as the brush border. The brush border is made up of cells containing villi that contain many microvilli which further maximise surface area & absorption. It directly comes into contact with the intestinal lumen

  • The 3 regions of the small intestine are:
      • Duodenum
    • Closest to the stomach
    • It is the shortest
    • Plenty of digestion & absorption occurs here
    • Surrounded by the large liver and pancreas. Although, these accessory organs never directly touch food
        • The pancreatic duct (from the pancreas) connects directly to the duodenum via the Sphincter of Oddi. The gallbladder (bile storage of liver via the common hepatic duct) connects to the pancreatic duct via the common bile duct, also leading to the duodenum
        The pancreas has many vital functions like secreting HCO3- and other digestive enzymes. In fact, 99% pancreatic cells are devoted to exocrine digestion (only 1% secretes endocrines)
      Jejunum
    • Located in the middle of the small intestine overall
    • Plenty of digestion & absorption also occurs here
      Ileum
    • Closest to the large intestine
    • It is the longest
    • Less absorption occurs here
