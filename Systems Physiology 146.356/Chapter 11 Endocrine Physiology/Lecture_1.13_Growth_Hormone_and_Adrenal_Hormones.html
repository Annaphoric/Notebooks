Lecture 1.13: Growth Hormone and Adrenal Hormones

    Growth Hormone
  • Growth hormone (GH) is secreted by the anterior pituitary gland. Its secretion is regulated by the hypotalamic hormone Growth Hormone Releasing Hormone (GHRH) and Somatostatin
      • GHRH up-regulates the release of GH; Somatostatin down-regulates the release of GH

  • Effects of GH
      • Stimulates epiphyseal plate hyperplastic growth located within osseus tissue (bone)
      Promotes the release of Insulin-like Growth Factor-1 (IGF-1) in the liver
    • This generally promotes mitotic cell divisions, specifically cyclins
      Increases amino acid uptake & ribosome production, inducing protein synthesis (especially in muscle)
      Promotes triglyceride breakdown and circulation of these energy stores
      Promotes gluconeogenesis in the liver ("anti-insulin effects")

  • Regulation of GH
      • GH's stimulating hormones GHRH & Somatostatin are affected by circadian rhythms
    • Most of these hormones are released during sleep
      Sex hormones, insulin & TH also play a role in affecting the release of GH
    • Most of these are released during adolescence & puberty
    Growth Hormone Related Disorders/Treatments
  • An indication of a GH-related disorder is abnormal levels GH and/or IGF-1
  • GH-Insensitivity Syndrome is a condition where GH receptors fail to respond to circulating GH even though it is still being released
  • Acromegaly is a condition where excess amount of GH is released during adulthood (most likely due to an anterior pituitary tumor). This leads to a sharp increase in bone size

  • GH-Replacement Therapy is commonly prescribed to children who have a documented GH deficiency
      • This consists of taking supplementary GH-pills
      Long term risks for taking external GH is not yet well understood, but should still be taken with care
    Cortisol
  • An adrenal hormone; also known as the stress hormone
  • Secreted mainly by cells located in the zona fasciculata of the adrenal cortex (medial part)
  • Small amounts are secreted in the zona reticularis (deepest part of the adrenal cortex)
  • It is stimulated by Adrenal Corticotropic hormone (ACTH) which is released from the anterior pituitary gland
  • Promotes blood pressure homeostasis via permissive effects of epinephrine & norepinephrine
      • Specifically, blood pressure is increased by constricting blood vessels
  • Regulates enzyme production for overall metabolism, especially metabolism that occurs in the liver
  • Maintains glucose at a homeostatic level

  • Has anti-inflammatory properties
      • It slows down the immune system by decreasing the production of immune-related cytokines like prostaglandins and capillary permeability to injured tissue
      It also suppresses the growth of WBCs
      It can be administered as a shot (cortisone shot) to relieve heavy inflammation usually caused by joint injuries that occur in an athletic event

  • Moderate levels of cortisol are required for these healthy bodily functions
      • Frequent high levels can have negative effects on overall health
    • This includes excess protein & fat degradation, gluconeogenesis
    • It can also cause menstrual cycle delays or decrease testosterone levels
    Cortisol-related Disorders/Treatments
  • Adrenal Insufficiency
      • Also referred to as Addison's Disease
      A disorder that causes low [cortisol]plasma & high [ACTH]plasma
    • Usually due to a malfunctioning adrenal gland or a disease that kills adrenal cells (like tuberculosis)
    • This can also arise from an autoimmune disorder of the adrenal gland
      Symptoms include weakness, fatigue, loss of appetite, weight loss, hypotension
      Treatments include orally taking synthetic adrenal hormones

  • Adrenal Excess
      • Also referred to as Cushing's Syndrome
      A disorder that causes high [cortisol] & low [ACTH] in blood plasma, leading to an increase in catabolism/breakdown of bone & muscle
    • Usually due to an adrenal tumor
      Symptoms include increased appetite, weight gain, increased blood sugar
    Aldosterone
  • An adrenal hormone secreted by cells in the zona glomerulosa of the adrenal cortex (most superficial part)
  • Acts as an anti-diuretic hormone by increasing [Na+]blood via water retention in the kidneys
    Epinephrine & Norepinephrine
  • The main sympathetic hormones that induce a "fight or flight" response
  • Secreted by cells located in the adrenal medulla
  • Functions include:
      • Promotes glycogenolysis (the breakdown of glycogen into its glucose subunits)
      Promotes lipid breakdown
      Increases blood pressure, heart rate & cardiac function
      Increases blood flow to the heart & muscles
      Decreases blood flow to the digestive & visceral (adipotic) organs
      Promotes respiration by dilating airways

  • There are 2 types of adrenergic receptors:
      • α-adrenergic receptors
    • Promotes vasoconstriction
      β-adrenergic receptors
    • Promotes vasodilation
      Both are metabotropic receptors
