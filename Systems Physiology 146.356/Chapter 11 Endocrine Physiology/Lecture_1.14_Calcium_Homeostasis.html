Lecture 1.14: Calcium Homeostasis

    Bone
  • Also referred to as osseus tissue
  • It is made up of primarily Ca2+PO43- (Calcium Phosphate)
  • 99% of Ca2+ in the body is found in bone

  • There are 3 types of cells that make up osseus tissue:
      • Osteocytes
      Osteoblasts
    • The cells that build bone
      Osteoclasts
    • The cells that break down bone

  • Extracellular Ca2+ is maintained at a homeostatic range by many hormones. Hormones that favour bone formation & increased bone mass include:
      • Insulin
      Growth Hormone
      IGF-1
      Estrogen
      Testosterone
      Calcitonin
  • Hormones that favour bone resorption (breakdown) & decreased bone mass include:
      • Parathyroid Hormone
      Cortisol
      Thyroid Hormones (T3/T4)
    Parathyroid Hormone
  • Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) is released when [Ca2+]blood is low. This low level of Ca2+ is the stimulus for the 4 parathyroid glands to release PTH

  • There are 4 main PTH responses:
      • An increase in osteoclast activity. Osteoclasts break down bone to liberate Ca2+ ions into the blood plasma
      An increase in absorption of Ca2+ ions in the intestines. In other words, more Ca2+ ions are extracted from food particles as they travel down the small intestines. Ca2+ is diffused from the intestinal walls and into blood vessels. This function is agonistic to the function of Vitamin D which is discussed next
      An increase in Ca2+ reabsorption in the kidneys. This allows less Ca2+ ions to be excreted as urine and more of it to enter the bloodstream from nephrotic tubules
      A decrease in the reabsorption of PO43- in the kidneys, excreting more PO43- ions. This, in turn, increases the amount of free Ca2+ ions for extracellular [Ca2+] homeostasis
    Vitamin D
  • Also classified as a hormone
  • Can be obtained via diet as D2 or D3 or via sunlight by converting 7-dehydrocholesterol into D3 in skin
  • Once vitamin D is deposited in the liver, it is hydroxylated once into 25-Hydroxy-D3 in the liver. Then, it travels to the kidneys to become hydroxylated once again into 1,25-Dihydroxy-D3 (with the help of 1-Hydroxylase, activated by PTH), which is the active form of vitamin D. The activated vit-D stimulates the transcription/translation of more Ca2+ channels located on intestinal epithelial cells. This causes the absorption of Ca2+ & PO43- ions into the blood

  • Since vit-D is a fat-soluble vitamin, it can be stored in adipose tissue

  • Research has shown that vit-D also strengthens innate immunity by promoting barrier integrity & microbiota in the gastrointestinal tract

  • Vitamin D deficiency causes the release of PTH as an attempt to increase [Ca2+]blood
    Calcitonin
  • A peptide hormone secreted by parafollicular cells in the thyroid gland
  • A high [Ca2+]blood is the stimulus for the secretion of calcitonin, which is uncommon. Although, this is also a stimulus for osteoblast activity, which deposits the Ca2+ ions in blood into osseus tissue. So, it is said that the role of calcitonin is to stimulate osteoblast activity
    Osteoporosis
  • The physiological condition that is caused by excess bone resorption (breakdown) and insufficient bone formation
  • This causes bone to be fragile and more prone to fractures
  • Likelihood of this condition increases with age, but it is most likely due to physical inactivity. Although this condition can occur with anyone, it is more likely to occur in women since they originally produce greater amounts of estrogen which protects bone but is lost as they age
