Lecture 2.9: Cardiac Pathologies

    Hypertension
  • Also referred to as high blood pressure
  • Normal blood pressure for an individual is 120/80 (mmHg) (120 SP, 80 DP)
  • High blood pressure is clinically characterised as having a BP of 140/90. Although recently, this value has been modified to 130/85, hinting further sales of hypertensive medications and/or earlier diagnoses or warning signs. A BP of 130/85 can also be defined as being in a pre-hypertensive state
  • Research has shown that 34% of the US population and 26% of the world population is hypertensive

  • An increase in afterload directly affects blood pressure proportionally

  • Over time, hypertension can cause to left ventricular hypertrophy (growth of tissue in size), leading to a decrease in contractile function & an increased likelihood of heart failure

  • There are 2 general types of hypertension:
      • Primary
    • More common than secondary
    • Characterised by an increase in total peripheral resistance due to a decrease of arteriole radius
    • Causes include obesity, insulin resistance, increased dietary salt intake, frequent stimulation of sympathetic drive (stress), salt-water retention
      Secondary
    • Less common than primary
    • Characterised by high blood pressure due to non-cardiovascular factors like renal hypertension (kidney malfunction, leading to an increase in extracellular fluid volume)

  • Treatments include:
      • Diuretics
    • Lowers blood volume & pressure by increasing amount of urine
      β-blockers
    • Decreases cardiac output by blocking β-adrenergic receptors on cardiomyocytes, acting as an adrenergic antagonist
      Ca2+ Channel Blockers
    • Induces vasodilation by decreasing Ca2+ influx in peripheral smooth muscle tissue, causing less contraction and decreasing TPR
    Heart Failure
  • Also referred to as congestive heart failure or dysfunction
  • Occurs when the heart does not pump an adequate amount of cardiac output. Baroreceptors also become desensitized to a change in BP, leading to a lower firing rate even when BP appears to be normal and resulting in a baroreceptor reflex which increases HR, TPR, sympathetic drive, ADH, aldosterone, BP
      • As aldosterone increases, the likelihood of pulmonary edema increases as well. Pulmonary edema is an adaptive response to a decrease in cardiac output, characterised by extracellular fluid buildup in the lungs & pulmonary vessels. Fluid retention also increases venous & capillary pressure

  • There are 2 types of heart failure:
      • Diastolic dysfunction
    • Caused by chronic hypertension and high afterload
    • The ventricle walls have a decreased compliance (or abnormal stiffness; thickening), causing the ventricle to not fill with blood properly. This results in a decreased EDV & SV (based on Frank-Starling's Law)
    • To treat diastolic dysfunction, the hypertension is first treated

      Systolic dysfunction
    • Caused by myocardial damage likely from a heart attack
    • Characterised by a decrease in coronary blood flow. Heart contractility is also decreased, leading to a lower SV for a given EDV (decreased ejection fraction; EF)
    • Can lead to further devastating heart attacks if not treated
    • To treat systolic dysfunction, the hypertension is first treated. Cardiac output may also be increased by adrenergic agonistic drugs inotropically
    Hypertropic Cardiomyopathy
  • An increase thickness of cardiomyocyte walls, especially in the interventricular septum & left ventricular wall
  • Characterised by an increase in cardiac output despite an increase in workload, leading to chest pains & diastolic heart failure due to slight coronary blockage. Most of the times, hypertropic cardiomyopathy is genetic

  • Studies have showed that there is a prevalence of 1 in 500 people and there is often no symptoms. There have been over 900 mutations in over 20 genes, including in myosin (specifically)

  • Treatments include drugs that prevent arrythmias and calm the heart. Strenuous exercise is also discouraged. If pains persist or hypertrophy becomes too advanced, a heart transplant is necessary

  • Generally hypertrophy is tissue growth due to an increase in cell size. There are 2 types of hypertrophy:
      • Concentric
    • Characterised by wider cells, causing tissue to be thicker and with smaller cavities
      Eccentric
    • Characterised by longer cells, causing tissue to be thinner and with larger cavities

  • There are 2 types of cardiac hypertrophy:
      • Physiological
    • Occurs naturally through chronic exercise or pregnancy
    • May result in enhanced cardiac function and slight chamber enlargement
    • No fibrosis occurs and gene expression is normal

      Pathological
    • Occurs through chronic hypertension, aortic stenosis (caused by aortic valve defect), or sarcomeric gene mutations
    • Results in cardiac dysfunction, fibrosis, myocyte necrosis and apoptosis
    • Associated with heart failure & increased mortality
    • Can lead to cardiac dilation, heart failure & worsening symptoms if not treated
    Myocardial Infarction
  • Also referred to as a heart attack
  • Caused by advanced cardiac ischemia (lack of blood flow) in coronary arteries, leading to heart damage. Lack of blood flow can arise due to plaque buildup in arteries. Plaque can be made of cholesterol, lipids, smooth muscle and other tissue proliferating

  • Symptoms include chest pain, the elevation of ST-segments in an EKG graph, and the prevalence of proteins like cardiac troponin in blood that shouldn't be there

  • Research shows that 1.1 million Americans suffer from a heart attack (due to v-fib; uncoordinated contractions) every year, and 40% of those people do not survive

  • MIs can be prevented by incorporating a healthy diet & exercise
  • Treatments for cardiac ischemia include blood thinners, diuretics, and/or β-blockers. Surgery like coronary artery bypass (CABG; creates a new pathway to supply blood to heart tissue using another vessel in the body) or a balloon angioplasty (opens up the affected artery by adding a stent) may be required to reduce symptoms and minimise the likelihood of another MI
    Preventing Most Cardiac Dysfunctions with Exercise
  • Exercise can:
      • Decrease heart O2 demand by lowering resting HR
      Increase the radius of coronary arteries
      Decrease the severity of hypertension & diabetes
      Decrease total plasma cholesterol
      Decrease the likelihood of blood clots
      Increase insulin sensitivity, leading to better blood glucose concentration regulatio
