Lecture 2.5: The Cardiac Cycle

    Introduction
  • The cardiac cycle consists of all the pressure changes, blood volume changes, electrical activity & heart sounds that occur in one complete heart beat. This process usually takes around 800ms

  • There are 2 periods of interest that occur in a cardiac cycle:
      • Systole
    • Refers to ventricular contraction
      Diastole
    • Refers to ventricular relaxation

      One cardiac cycle consists of 5 more specific steps in order:
  • Late Ventricular Filling
  • Isovolumetric Contraction
  • Ventricular Ejection
  • Isovoluemtric Relaxation
  • Early Ventricular Filling
    Late Ventricular Filling
  • This is the first step of the cardiac cycle which occurs towards the end of ventricular filling
  • In this step:
      • Atrioventricular (AV) valves are open, allowing both the right & left ventricles fill with blood
      Semilunar valves are closed
      Atrial pressure is slightly higher than ventricular pressure, keeping the AV valves open
  • Ventricular pressure slowly increases as it fills with blood
  • Although, the left ventricular pressure will always be higher than the right ventricular pressure
      • Aortic & pulmonary arterial pressure is slowly decreasing
      The SA node becomes depolarised right before the end of ventricular filling, causing atrial depolarisation & contraction and forming the P-wave
  • At this point, the ventricles finish filling. The current volume at this point in time is known as the End Diastolic Volume (EDV). This is the greatest volume that the ventricles can have
      • The AV valves close right before the next step, making the "lub" sound

  • Generally, the left & right atria are always filling with blood because there is no valve that regulates atrial blood volume
    Isovolumetric Contraction
  • This is the second step of the cardiac cycle
  • "Isovolumetric" means "no change" or "constant" "volume"
  • In this step:
      • All valves are normally, fully closed to prevent backflow of blood and allowing blood volume in both atria & ventricles to remain constant
      Ventricles become depolarised while the atria are repolarising, forming the QRS-complex
      Ventricular pressure increases exponentially, becoming much higher than atrial pressure but still not as high as aortic until the end of contraction
    Ventricular Ejection
  • This is the third step in the cardiac cycle
  • In this step:
      • The ventricles finish contracting, forcing the semilunar valves to open
      Ventricular pressure as at its highest point, slightly higher than aortic pressure & much greater than atrial pressure
      Blood volume within the ventricles is rapidly decreasing because blood is being ejected through the semilunar valves, away from the heart. This continues until the ventricles reach a certain volume known as the End Systolic Volume (ESV). This is the lowest volume that the ventricles can have
      The ventricles repolarise towards the end of this phase, forming the T-wave
      The semilunar valves close right before the next step, making the "dub" sound

  • The volume witin the ventricles can never be zero; instead, ESV is around 40% of EDV. The amount of blood that left the heart is known as the Stroke Volume (SV). This is the difference between EDV & ESV
  • An exact fraction or percentage of how much blood left the ventricles during this time relative to EDV is referred to as the Ejection Fraction (EF). This is calculated by the formula: EF = SV / EDV.
      • EF is around 55-65% at rest & 70-75% during exercise
      An EF of under 40% indicates heart failure
      An EF that is unusually, consistently high indicates enlarged ventricular muscle cells or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
    Isovolumetric Relaxation
  • This is the fourth step of the cardiac cycle
  • In this step:
      • All valves are normally, fully closed to prevent backflow to blood and allowing the blood volume in both the atria & ventricles to remain constant
      Ventricular pressure becomes lower than aortic presure but still higher than atrial pressure
      The ventricles finish repolarising
    Early Ventricular Filling
  • This step is the early phase of the first step (or the last step)
  • In this step:
      • The AV valves open, allowing the ventricles to fill with blood
      Blood volume exponentially moves towards EDV
  • If heart rate were to increase, the time it takes for the ventricles to fill with blood would be shortened
      • Ventricular pressure becomes lower than atrial pressure & much lower than aortic pressure
      This period of time marks the TP-interval
