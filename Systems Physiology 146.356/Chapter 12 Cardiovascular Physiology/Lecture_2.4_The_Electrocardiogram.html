Lecture 2.4: The Electrocardiogram

    Introduction
  • The electrocardiogram (EKG or ECG) is a graphical recording of the electrical activity of the heart. It only reads the depolarisation & repolarisation that occurs per cardiac cycle

  • An ECG measures the sum of all the electrical activity (mainly atrial/ventricular action potentials) in the heart for every moment in time. The magnitudes of peaks & troughs shown in a normal ECG is dependent on the location of the leads (electrode pairs where the anode is (-) charged & the cathode is (+) charged). The direction of the lead always occurs from (-) -> (+) (demonstrating e- flow) where an anode is the reference electrode & the cathode is the recording electrode

  • There are 3 main limb leads:
      • Lead I
        Measured from an anode placed on the right wrist to a cathode placed on the left wrist
      Lead II
        Measured from an anode placed on the right wrist to a cathode placed on the left ankle
        Most accurately depicts an ECG graph
      Lead III
    • Measured from an anode placed on the left wrist to a cathode placed on the left ankle

  • A ground lead is also placed on the right ankle to act as a ground wire
    The ECG Graph
  • There are 3 distinct peaks of any ECG graph:
      • P-wave
    • Represents atrial depolarisation
      QRS-complex
    • Represents ventricular depolarisation & atrial repolarisation simoultaneously
      T-wave
    • Represents ventricular repolarisation

  • Important regions of an ECG graph include:
      • PQ-Interval
    • Respresents the activity between the P-wave & Q-trough
      PR-Interval
    • Represents the activity between the P-wave & the R-peak
      QT-Interval
    • Represents the activity between the Q-trough & the T-wave
      ST-Segment
    • Represents the activity between the S-trough & the T-wave
      TP-Interval
    • Represents the activity between the T-wave & the P-wave of the next cardiac cycle
      RR-Interval
    • Represents the activity between the R-peak to the R-peak of the next cardiac cycle

  • Various pieces of electrical information can be obtained by performing an ECG including:
      • Heart Rate
    • Measured as Rate = 60 / (R-R Interval)
    • Diagnoses include normal heart rate, bradycardia (unusally slow heart rate) or tachycardia (unusually fast heart rate)
      Cardiac Rhythm
    • Measured by observing the distances between subsequent RR-Intervals
    • Diagnoses include normal rhythm, cardiac arrhythmias (unbalanced heart rhythmn), irregular heart beats, or cardiac ischemia (minor blockage of coronary arteries)
        • Note: Diagnoses usually require additional, follow-up tests

  • Mechanical information like force or strength of the heart and blood volume cannot be determined using an ECG graph
    Cardiac Refractory Period

    Heart Abnormalities based on ECG Graph
  • Types of heart abnormalities include:
      • An additional P-wave
    • Indicates AV nodal damage and a 2nd degree heart block
      Uncontrolled prevalence of peaks
    • Indicates ventricular fibrillation which requires a defibrillator or other immediate treatment in order to keep affected person alive
      Long QT-syndrome
    • Indicates prolonged ventricular APs due to loss of Kr/Ks expression
    • This is a sign of an irregular heartbeat or cardiac arrhythmia
    • In most cases, it is a congenital condition
      Elevated ST segment
    • May be associated with coronary ischemia, which indicates an increased risk of a myocardial infarction (MI; heart attack)
