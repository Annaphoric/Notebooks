Lecture 4.2: COVID-19

    Viruses
  • Viruses are infectious particles containing genetic material encapsulated by peptides and may even have lipid membrane
      • Their genetic material may be double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), single-stranded DNA (ssDNA), double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) or single-stranded RNA (ssRNA)
      The genetic material is surrounded by many small protein molecules known as capsomeres which make up the capsid
      Many viruses, but not all, have a lipid membrane known as a lipid envelope. They may also have glycoproteins which act as ligands to bind to a host's specific receptor
      They lack many organelles that prokaryotes and/or eukaryotes have

  • They are not considered alive by many biologists since they cannot reproduce on their own or metabolise, but this is debatable

      • How an virus invades a host cell:
    1. When a virus finds a cell to invade, it attaches its glyocoprotein onto a receptor, releases its lipid envelope & endocytotically enters the cell
    2. The virus releases it genetic material into the cell which becomes incorporated into the host cell's DNA
    3. At the appropriate time, the host's enzymes replicates, transcribes, and translates the viral DNA, forming new viral components
    4. The viral components assemble to create many new viruses
    5. The viruses forcibly leaves the cell, causing it to lyse
    Coronaviruses
  • Coronaviruses are ssRNA viruses that have nucleocapsid proteins, a lipid envelope, envelope proteins, membrane glycoproteins & spike proteins

  • "Corona" comes from the crown-like appearance under an electron microscope

  • Their genome encodes for proteins that are responsible for host infection, membrane fusion, viral assembly, RNA to DNA conversion (reverse transcriptase) and so on

  • Their spike proteins protrude from the lipid envelope. They facilitate viral entry by recognising a host cell
      • The spike protein (S protein) is a transmembrane, clove-shaped protein divided into 3 segments:
    • S1 subunit or the large ectodomain
        • Contains the globular receptor-binding domain (RBD) and membrane fusion subunit (S2)
    • Single pass transmembrane
    • Intracellular tail
    SARS-CoV-2
  • The novel coronavirus first reported in December 2019 in Wuhan, China. As of right now (December 2020; a year later), this virus has rapidly spread to almost every region of the world, causing the global pandemic of COVID-19

  • This virus binds to Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme-2 (ACE2) found on various types of cells. Specifically, the RBD of the spike glycoprotein binds to the non-catalytic (non-functional) side of the ACE2 receptor
      • Typically, the response to binding to this receptor involves RAAS. As mentioned in the endocrinology chapter, RAAS initiates with angiotensinogen. This protein is cleaved by renin into angiotensin I (1-10). Then, ACE converts Angiotensin I into Angiotension II (1-8). Angiotensin II binds to AT-1 receptors, which results in an increase in BP, enhanced systemic vasoconstriction, hypertrophy, & pro-inflammatory effects
      It was recently discovered that ACE2 is able to convert Angiotensin I to Angiotensin 1-9 and/or Angiotensin II to Angiotensin 1-7. Angiotensin 1-9 can be converted to Angiotensin 1-7. Angiotensin 1-7 beings to Mas receptors, which results in antagonist effects as in RAAS
      ACE2 is most in nearly every organ but is most abundant in type II alveolar cells, and somewhat in the oral & nasal cavities

  • Physiological Host Responses include (mild or moderate; occurs 80% of cases):
      • Viral entry and early responses
    • Infects a target cell via ACE2 and TMPRSS2
    • Actively replicates and releases more viruses via pyroptosis (a type of apoptosis)

      Host immune responses
    • Clears and recruits macrophages & dendritic cells, then cytotoxic T-cells
    • Recognises DAMPs and PAMPs
    • Promotes inflammatory effects by releasing cytokines & chemokines
    • Eliminates other infected cells

  • Pathogenic Host Responses include (severe; 20% of cases):
      • Hyperinflammatory Phase
    • Induces a cytokine storm (a systemic overproduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines) involving the release of IL-6, IL-10, IL-2, IP-10, G-CSF, IFNy, TNF (Tumor necrosis factor), & M1P1a
    • Excessively infiltrates immune cells within the lungs
      Multi-organ Dysfunction
    • Leads to extra-pulmonary involvement (organ failure)
    • Elicits a procoagulant response (excessive clotting; DIC)
    Systemic Effects of COVID-19
  • Regarding the nervous system:
      • 50% of severe patients suffer from a complication in this system
      Milder complications are more common
    • They include gustatory & olfactory (taste & smell) dysfunctions due to direct epithelial damage
      There is evidence that COVID-19 invades the brain, most likely due to hypoxia

  • Regarding the cardiovascular system:
      • Hypercoagulation is common in severe patients due to excessive inflammatory effects. This involves excessive production or activity for the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin and fibrinogen to fibrin
      There is an elevation of plasma cardiac troponin, which is normally an indication of a heart attack. Although, the elevation is due to excessive release of cytokines rather than myocardial damage

  • Regarding the renal system:
      • Proteinuria may occur in severe patients due to tubular necrosis at the filtration membrane

  • Regarding the digestive system:
      • There is an elevation of liver enzymes, indicating liver damage and/or inflammation
      May induce acute diabetes, indicating exocrine panreatic injury
      Intestinal enterocytes may also become infected

  • Regarding the reproductive system:
      • When comparing pregnant women to non-pregnant women, there appears no difference in mortality rate
      When comparing males to females, females are less susceptible to COVID-19 infection and effects than men. This infers that estrogen may cause a lower expression of ACE2 receptors, giving females slightly more protection
