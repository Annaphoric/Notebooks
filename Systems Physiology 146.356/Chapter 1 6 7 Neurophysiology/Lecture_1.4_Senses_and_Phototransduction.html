Lecture 1.4: Senses and Phototransduction

    Sensory Receptors
  • These receptors convert an external stimulus into receptor/local potentials (LPs) and eventually into an action potentials (APs)
  • They are located on the peripheral ends of an afferent neuron
      • A neuron that sends a signal from the axon terminal to the nucleus
  • There are 2 types:
      • Receptor-cell mediated
    • Includes a receptor cell between the external stimulus & afferent neuron
        • The receptor cell sends LPs to its afferent neuron which can lead to an AP
    • Occurs in vision, taste, & auditory stimuli
      Non-receptor-cell mediated
    • Does not include a receptor cell between the external stimulus & afferent neuron
    • Occurs in olfactory stimuli
    Vision
  • There are 5 types of cells located on the retina (back of the eye)
      • Photoreceptor cells
    • Cells that receive light stimuli and send signals to bipolar cells
    • Includes rods & cones
    • Mainly permeable to Na+ & Ca2+ ions, increasing their resting membrane potential (RMP) to around -35mV
    • Most posterior
      Horizontal cells
      Bipolar cells
      Amacrine cells
      Ganglion cells
    • Cells that are connected to and interact with the optic nerve (their axon)
    • They fire an AP that travels toward the optic nerve
    • Most anterior
    Organisation of Cells within the Retina
  • Photoreceptors are depolarised (having a potential difference of around -35mV) at rest
  • Molecules/peptides that play a role in phototransduction include:
      • Rhodopsin
    • A photopigment made up of opsin & retinal (light-sensitive molecule) located on rods & cones
      Transducin
      Guanylyl Cyclase
      Cyclic GMP
      Cyclic GMP phosphodiesterase
      Non-selective Cation Channels
    Transduction
      In the dark:
    1. Guanylyl cyclase converts GTP to cGMP
    2. cGMP acts as a ligand to continuously bind to non-selective cation channels, keeping them open. This allows Na+ & Ca2+ ions to diffuse into the cell
    3. Photoreceptors become slightly depolarised (having a RMP of around -35mV)

      In the light:
    1. Light hits rhodopsin, causing the photo-rearrangement of cis-retinal into trans-retinal. This changes the overall structure of rhodopsin
    2. Opsin activates transducin
    3. Transducin promotes the synthesis of cGMP phosphodiesterase. This enzyme cleaves a Pi group from cGMP, deactivating it
    4. Non-selective cation channels close due to the decrease of intracellular cGMP
    5. Photoreceptors become more hyperpolarised (having a RMP of around -70mV)
