Lecture 1.3: Synapses

    Synaptic Transmissions
  • A synapse is the communication made within a cell-to-cell junction
  • There are 2 types of synapses:
      • Electrical
    • Current flows through a gap junction connected between 2 cells
    • Not as numerous as chemical synapses, except in cardiac muscle cells

      Chemical
    • Neurotransmitters flow through a synaptic cleft from one cell to another
    • Numerous when involving neurons

  • A cellular response that opens K+ channels causes an inhibitory (hyperpolarisable) response
  • A cellular response that opens Na+ channels causes an excitatory (depolarisable) response

  • There are 2 types of post-synaptic (ligand-gated) receptors:
      • Ionotropic
    • A receptor that also acts as a to allow the influx of ions
    • Cellular responses are quick and short-lived
      Metabotropic
    • A receptor that causes a signaling cascade that includes a 2° messenger
    • Cellular responses are slow and long-lasting
    • Also known as GTP-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs)
    Mechanism of Chemical Synapses
  1. An action potential propagates from the body of a pre-synaptic neuron to its axon terminals
  2. Ca+ ions rush into the axon terminal of the pre-synaptic neuron through voltage-gated Ca+ channels
  3. Ca+ ions help fuse the vesicles within the axon terminal onto the pre-synaptic membrane, releasing their neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft
  4. These neurotransmitter bind to post-synaptic dendritic receptors
  5. The post-synaptic neuron undergoes a cellular response which can be either excitatory/depolarisable or inhibitory/hyperpolarisable
  6. Neurotransmitters are removed and recycled from the synaptic cleft
    How Drugs Affect Chemical Synapses
  • There are many ways drugs can affect synapse depending on their stucture/chemistry. Some examples include:
      • Increasing the leakage of neurotransmitters from vesicles to the pre-synaptic cytoplasm, causing them to be degraded by enzymes
      Inhibiting or stimulating neurotransmitter release, synthesis, metabolism or reuptake
      Binding to a post-synaptic recepter to block (antagonist behaviour) or mimic (agonistic behaviour) the neurotransmitter itself
      Causing the inhibition or stimulation of 2° messengers

  • Mechanism of Cocaine
      • Blocks the reuptake transporter protein that alows dopamine to be recycled from the synaptic cleft into the pre-synaptic neuron
    • This allows dopamine to remain within the synaptic cleft, bind to the post-synaptic neuron more frequently, and result in more frequent excitatory responses
    • As a result, new receptors are formed on the post-synaptic membrane, causing the feeling of withdrawal and dependency

  • Mechanism of Antidepressants
      • Similar to cocaine, these drugs block a reuptake transporter protein. The difference is that serotonin is the target neurotransmitter instead of dopamine
      Common antidepressants include:
    • Zoloft
    • Prozac
      These drugs are known as Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)

  • Mechanism of Anti-Anxiety Medications
      • Promotes the binding of GABA onto a post-synaptic receptor or mimics its mechanism
      Since GABA is an inhibitory neurotransmitter and anti-anxiety medications are GABA-agonists, the post-synaptic neuron will be less likely to excite
    • This allows a person to feel more calm more frequently
      GABAA is an ionotopic receptor that allows that influx of Cl- ions
      Examples of anti-anxiety medications include:
    • Benzodiazepines like Valium & Xanax

  • Mechanism of Opioids
      • Inhibits a modulatory interneuron's (neuroglial cell) inhibitory effect of a pre-synaptic neuron containing dopamine within its vesicles
    • This allows the pre-synaptic neuron to become more excitable and more likely to release its dopamine into the synaptic cleft, allowing more of its binding to the post-synaptic receptor
