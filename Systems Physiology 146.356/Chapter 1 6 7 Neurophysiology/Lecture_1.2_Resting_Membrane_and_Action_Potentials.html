Lecture 1.2: Resting, Local & Action Potentials

    Distribution of Ions
  • Normally, [Na+] is higher outside the cells than inside while the [K+] is higher inside the cell
  • Nerve cells are most permeable to K+

  • Ions move across the membrane through 5 main channels/transporters:
      • Sodium/Potassium Pump
    • Pumps 2 K+ ions into the cell for every 3 Na+ ions pumped out
      Sodium leak channels
      Potassium leak channels
      Sodium voltage-gated channels
      Potassium voltage-gated channels
      The last 4 channels are found in around a 1:20:5:5 ratio in abundance on a neuron (with K+ channels being the most abundant; thus the membrane is most permeable to K+)

  • Leak channels are always open, especially K+ channels, thereby giving the inside of the cells a net negative charge in the membrane
      • The Na+/K+ pump deposits K+ ions back into the cell but isn't as abundant as the K+ leak channels so the net negativity still remains
  • Depolarisation is the process of increasing positivity in the membrane
  • Repolarisation is the process of increasing negativity in the membrane
  • The greater the difference in concentrations, the greater the driving force of diffusion

  • Hyperkalemia
      • A condition where the [K+]blood is consistently higher than normal
    • This causes a neuron to become more excitable/depolarisable due to an elevated resting membrane potential
        • Tremors, twitches, and irregular heartbeats can occur as a result
  • Hypernatremia
      • A condition where the [Na+]blood is consistently higher than normal
    • This causes an increase in a neuron action potential's spike height
    Voltage values of Membrane Potentials in most neurons
  • Resting Potential
      • Approximately -70mV
  • Threshold Potential
      • Approximately -55mV
  • Peak Potential
      • Approximately +30mV
    2 Types of Potentials Fired by Neurons
  • Action Potential
      • The event of propagating an electrical signal down an axon to communicate to other cells (whether it be other neurons or muscle cells)
      Always causes depolarisation
      Stimulated by the opening of voltage-gated channels
      Its magnitude is independent of the strength of the stimulus, instead it is dependent on the frequency of the stimulus (which is high)
      Does not decrement with distance traveled along the axon
    • This is due to the presence of myelin sheaths & nodes of Ranvier
      They cannot sum up due to the implementation of a refractory period

  • Local/Graded/Receptor/Synaptic Potential
      • The event of stimulating an excitatory or inhibitory reponse without causing an action potential
      Can cause either depolarisation or hyperpolarisation
      Stimulated by ligands/messengers binding to a ligand-gated channel
      Its magnitude is dependent on the strength (amplitude) of the stimulus
      Decrements with distance traveled along the axon
      They can sum up when stimulated at close enough times to each other
