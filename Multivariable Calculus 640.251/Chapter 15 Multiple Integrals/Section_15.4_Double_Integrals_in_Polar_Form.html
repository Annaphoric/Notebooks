Section 15.4: Double Integrals in Polar Form

    Integrals in Polar Coordinates
  • Double integrals are sometimes easier to evaluate if they are converted from Cartesian form to polar form

  • Suppose function f(r, θ) is bound by region R = {θ ∈ [α, β], r ∈ [g(θ), h(θ)]} && {0 <= g(θ) <= h(θ) <= a}. The region R would lie within region Q = {r ∈ [0, a] for θ ∈ [α, β]} where (β - α) ∈ [0, 2π]. Each division of angles is given by θ = α + m Δθ leading up to θ = β where m is location of the polar rectangle. Based on this information, it is revealed that:
      • The change of angle (Δθ; angular displacement) is given by:
    • Δθ = (β - α) / m
      The change in radius Δr is the highest value for the radius (a) divided by m:
    • Δr = a / m
      The change in area ΔAk of any polar rectangle is found by multiplying Δθ, Δr, & the radius at k
      The sum SN of the all the polar rectangles is written as:
    • SN = ∑k = 1N(f(rk, θk)) ΔAk
      Taking the limit as N approaches infinity defines the double integral of the polar function:
    • limN ➝ ∞(SN) = ∬R(f(rk, θk)) dAk
      The iterated integral of the double integral is written as:
    • ∫αβ(∫g(θ)h(θ)(r) dr) dθ
  • If the function f(r, θ) = 1, then the area of the f bounded by region R is:
      • A = ∬R(r) dr dθ
  • Cartesian functions like f(x, y) can be converted to polar functions like f(r, θ) using the following equations:
      • r2 = x2 + y2
      x = r cosθ
      y = r sinθ
      dx dy = r dr dθ
    Finding Limits of Integration
  • To find the limits of integration in polar form, a sketch can be made to give a visual representation of the bounded function. The r-limits can be found by determining the curve closest to the origin (lower curve) & the curve farthest from the origin (upper curve) at a certain θ. The θ-limits can be found by locating the lowest & highest θ-values possible that bounds the region
  • Find the limits of integration for the function f(r, θ) bounded by the region R = {r = 1 + cosθ & r = 1}.

      • Solving for the angles of intersection:
    • 1 = 1 + cosθ
    • 0 = cosθ
    • θ = {-π/2, π/2}
      Within these angles, the lower curve is found to be r = 1 and the upper curve is r = {1 + cosθ}
      Therefore, the integral can be written as:
    • ∫-π/2π/2(∫11 + cosθ(r f(r, θ)) dr) dθ
    Example 2:
      Find the area enclosed within the lemniscate r2 = 4 cos(2θ).

    1. The area of the lemniscate located within one quadrant is exactly a quarter of the entire region of the lemiscate. This means the area located within a quadrant can be multiplied by 4 to solve for the total area enclosed by the lemiscate. Solving for the r-bounds:
        2. r = 2 sqrt(cos(2θ))
        r in [0, 2 sqrt(cos(2θ))]

    2. Solving for the θ-bounds (for r = 0):
        3. 0 = 2 sqrt(cos(2θ))
        0 = cos(2θ)
        θ = π/4
        θ in [0, π/4]

    3. The area A of the lemniscate is:
        4. A = 4 ∫0π/4(∫02 sqrt(cos(2θ))(r) dr) dθ
        A = 2 ∫0π/4(2 sqrt(cos(2θ))2) dθ
        A = 8 ∫0π/4cos(2θ) dθ
        A = 4 [sin(2θ)]0π/4
        A = 4
    Example 3:
      Evaluate {∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx} where R is the semicircular region bounded by y = 0 & y = sqrt(1 - x2).

    1. The given Cartesian bounds can be converted into their polar form:
        2. r sinθ = 0; r2 sin2θ = 1 - r2 cos2θ
        θ = {0, π}; r2 = 1
        θ = {0, π}; r = {0, 1};

    2. The given Cartesian function can be converted into a polar function:
        3. ex2 + y2 = er2

    3. Evaluating the integral:
        4. ∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx = ∫∫R(er2 r) dr dθ
        ∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx = 1/2 ∫0π([er2]01) dθ
        ∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx = 1/2 ∫0π(e - 1) dθ
        ∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx = (e - 1)/2 [θ]0π
        ∫∫R(ex2 + y2) dy dx = π (e - 1)/2
    Example 4:
      Evaluate the integral ∫01(∫0sqrt(1 - x2)(x2 + y2) dy) dx.
    1. Converting the Cartestian bounds (y-limits) to a polar form:
        2. x = 0; y = sqrt(1 - x2);
        r cosθ = 0; r sinθ = sqrt(1 - (r cosθ)2);
        r in [0, 1]; θ in [0, π/2];
    2. let V = ∫01(∫0sqrt(1 - x2)(x2 + y2) dy) dx. The integral is rewritten then evaluated as shown:
        3. V = ∫0π/2(∫01(r3) dr) dθ
        V = 1/4 ∫0π/2(1) dθ
        V = π/8
    Example 5:
      Find the volume V of the solid region bounded above by the paraboloid z = {9 - x2 - y2} and below by the unit circle C = {x2 + y2 = 1, z = 0}.

    1. Changing the function to a polar form:
        2. 9 - x2 - y2
        9 - r2(cos2θ + sin2θ)
        9 - r2

    2. Given the unit circle, it can be observed that the radius r extends from 0 to 1 & the angle θ changes from 0 to 2π. These are the respective r-bounds & θ-bounds; therefore, the volume of the solid can be found by evaluating the polar integral is evaluated as:
        3. V = ∫02π(∫01(9r - r3) dr) dθ
        V = ∫02π([9r2/2 - r4/4]01) dθ
        V = ∫02π(9/2 - 1/4) dθ
        V = 17/4 [θ]02π
        V = 17π/2
    Example 6:
      Find the area of the region R enclosed by the circle C = {x2 + y2 = 4, z = 0}, bound by the lower line {y = 1} and upper line {y = x sqrt(3)}.
