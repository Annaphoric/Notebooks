Section 15.7: Cylindrical & Spherical Coordinates

    Cylindrical Coordinates
  • Cylindrical coordinates are a combination of polar coordinates & rectangular coordinates. They have the form (r, θ, z) where r is the distance from a point within the xy-plane tothe origin (same as in polar form), θ is the angle from the positive x-axis increasing counter-clockwise when observing from the positive z-axis (same as in polar form), and z is the position of a point along the z-axis from the origin (rectangular vertical coordinate)

  • The equations relating cylindrical coordinates to rectangular coordinates are the same as those with polar coordinates:
      • x = r cosθ
      y = r sinθ
      r2 = x2 + y2
      tanθ = y/x
      z = z

  • The equation r = 1 in cylindrical form is a cylinder of radius 1 for z = all real numbers. The equation r = 0 is the x-axis
    Integration in Cylindrical Form
  • To compute the volume of a given region D in cylindrical coordinates, the region is partitioned into cylindrical wedges. For the k-th wedge, the respective dimensions are Δrk, Δθk, Δzk. The sum of all every wedge that makes up region D is the Riemann sum SN of wedges where N is the number of wedges. The triple integral is revealed as the number of wedges approaches infinity. Given a function f(r, θ, z) bounded by D, the Riemann sum and triple integral are written as:
      • SN = ∑k = 1N(f(rk, θk, zk) rk) Δrk Δθk Δzk
      limN ➝ ∞(SN) = ∭D(f(r, θ, z)) dV
      limN ➝ ∞(SN) = ∭D(f(r, θ, z) r) dr dθ dz

  • The θ-limits of integration are restricted from zero to 2π from one angle to another. The lowest possible limit for r is zero
    Example 1:
      Find the limits of integration in cylindrical coordinates for integrating the function f(r, θ, z) over region D = {z = 0, z = x2 + y2, x2 + (y - 1)2 = 1}.

    1. The z-limits are z = [0, r2]. To find the r-limits, the third equation is converted to a polar form:
        2. x2 + y2 - 2y + 1 = 1
        r2 = 2r sinθ
        r = {0, 2 sinθ}

    2. Solving for the θ-limits:
        3. 0 = 2 sinθ
        θ = {0, π}

    3. The integral is written as:
        4. ∫0π(∫02 sinθ(∫0r2(f(r, θ, z) r) dz) dr) dθ
    Example 2:
      Find the centroid (centre of mass) of the solid bounded laterally by the cylinder {x2 + y2 = 4} and vertically by {z = 0, x2 + y2}.

    1. Converting the bound equations to a polar form:
        2. z = {0, r2} (z-limits); r = {0, 2} (r-limits); θ = {0, 2π} (θ-limits)

    2. Solving for the mass m given the density equal to one:
        3. m = ∫02π(∫02(∫0r2(r) dz) dr) dθ
        m = ∫02π(∫02(r3) dr) dθ
        m = 4 ∫02π(1) dθ
        m = 8π

    3. Since the centroid lies in the centre with respect to x & y, the x and y coordinates of the centre of mass are both zero. To solve for the z-coordinate, the first z-moment (Mxy) is is found:
        4. Mxy = ∫02π(∫02 r(∫0r2(z) dz) dr) dθ
        Mxy = 1/2 ∫02π(∫02(r5) dr) dθ
        Mxy = 64/12 ∫02π(1) dθ
        Mxy = 32π/3

    4. Solving for the z-coordinate c of the centroid:
        5. c = Mxy / m
        c = (32π/3) / (8π)
        c = 4/3
        Therefore the centroid is the point (0, 0, 4/3)
    Spherical Coordinates
  • These coordinates locate points using 1 distance measurement and 2 angle measurements unlike cylindrical (which uses 2 distance & 1 angle measurement). It has the form (ρ, φ, θ). The first coordinate ρ is the distance from a point to the origin (can never be (-)). The second coordinate φ is the angle displaced from the z-axis (can only be from 0 to π). The third coordinate θ is the angle displaced from the x-axis, increasing counter-clockwise towards the y-axis when looking for the positive z-axis (can only be from 0 to 2π).

  • Equations relating Spherical Coordinates to Cartesian & Cylindrical Coordinates:
      • x = r cosθ = ρ cosθ sinφ
      y = r sinθ = ρ sinθ cosφ
      z = z = ρ cosφ
      x2 + y2 = r2 = ρ2 sin2φ
      x2 + y2 + z2 = r2 + z2 = ρ2

  • The equation ρ = 1 is a sphere with a radius of one. The equation φ = π/3 is a cone opening up towards the (+) z-axis. The equation θ = π/3 is a half-plane originating from the z-axis
    Example 3:
  • Convert the Cartesian equation {x2 + y2 + (z - 1)2 = 1} into a spherical form.

      • Simplifying and making the appropriate substitutions:
    • x2 + y2 + z2 - 2z + 1 = 1
    • ρ2 - 2ρ cosφ = 0
    • ρ = 2 cosφ
    Example 4:
  • Convert the Cartesian equation {z2 = x2 + y2} into a spherical form.

      • Making the appropriate substitutions & simplifying:
    • ρ2 cos2φ = ρ2 sin2φ
    • cosφ = sinφ
    • φ = π/4
    Integration in Spherical Form
  • Assume spherical function f(ρ, φ, θ) is bounded by region D made up of N spherical wedges. The k-th wedge has the dimensions: Δρk, Δφk, & Δθk. The Riemann sum SN is the sum of every wedge's volume. The triple integral is expressed by letting N approach infinity. These are written as:
      • SN = ∑k = 1N(f(ρk, φk, θk) ρk2 sinφk) Δρk Δφk Δθk
      limN ➝ ∞(SN) = ∭D(f(ρ, φ, θ) ρ2 sinφ) dρ dφ dθ
    Example 5:
  • Find the volume of the ice cream cone made by region D = {ρ = 1, φ = π/3}.

      • The limits of integration are:
    • ρ = {0, 1}; φ = {0, π/3}; θ = {0, 2π}

      The volume V of the ice cream cone is:
    • V = ∫02π(∫0π/3(∫01(ρ2 sinφ) dρ) dφ) dθ
    • V = 1/3 ∫02π(∫0π/3(sinφ) dφ) dθ
    • V = -1/3 ∫02π(cos(π/3) - cos(0)) dθ
    • V = -1/3 ∫02π(1/2 - 1) dθ
    • V = π/3
    Example 6:
      A solid of density δ = 1 occupies same ice cream cone region as the previous example. Find the solid's z-moment of inertia.

    1. The square of the distance from the z-axis to the solid is {x2 + y2}. In spherical coordinates, this distance is written as ρ2 sin2φ. The limits of integration remain the same. Therefore, the integral for the inertial z-moment (Iz) is written as:
        2. Iz = ∫02π(∫0π/3(∫01((ρ2sin2φ)(ρ2sinφ)) dρ) dφ) dθ
        Iz = ∫02π(∫0π/3(sin3φ ∫01((ρ4) dρ) dφ) dθ
        Iz = 1/5 ∫02π(∫0π/3(sin3φ) dφ) dθ
        Iz = 1/5 ∫02π(∫0π/3((1 - cos2φ)sinφ) dφ) dθ
        Iz = -1/5 ∫02π([cosφ - cos3φ/3]0π/3) dθ
        Iz = -1/5 ∫02π((1/2 - 1) - (1/8 - 1)/3) dθ
        Iz = -1/5 ∫02π(7/24 - 1/2) dθ
        Iz = 1/24 ∫02π(1) dθ
        Iz = π/12
