Section 12.1: 3D Coordinate Systems

    Right-Hand Rule
  • The proper way to draw a 3D-CG (3D-Coordinate Graph) is to use the appropriate parts of your hands to point towards a (+) axis
      • The thumb points towards the z+ axis
      The other fingers point toward the x+ axis
      The palm points towards the y+ axis
  • A (+) angle moves counter-clockwise when the fingers curl from the x-axis towards the y-axis
    3D Coordinate Graph
  • This type of graph is also known as a Cartesian Coordinate Graph or Rectangular Coordinate Graph
  • Points located on an axis have coordinates from the other planes equal to 0. For example, if a point is located on the x-axis, it will have a y-coordinate & z-coordinate equal to 0 and will have a Cartesian/rectangular coordinate of (x, 0, 0) where x is the magnitude of the point from the origin having a direction
    Planes on a 3D Graph
  • Every plane on a 3D graph has a standard equation associated with it
      • The xy-plane has an equation of z = 0
      The xz-plane has an equation of y = 0
      The yz-plane has an equation of x = 0
  • Every section of space between planes is known as an octant (analogous to quadrants on a 2D graph)
      • There are 8 octants in total
    • The first octant is the region in which all axes point towards the (+) direction
  • A combination of any 2 planes leads to a line on the graph, parallel to the unspecified axis
  • A combination of any 3 planes leads to a point on the graph
    Equations and Inequalites on a 3D Graph
  • A simple equation like x = 2 signifies that there must be a yz-plane going towards ∞ located at a distance of 2 on the x-axis from the origin
      • This means that the value of y & z can be any real number within (-∞, ∞)
  • A simple inequality like z ≥ 3 indicates the entire region of space above the value of 3
      • This also means that the value of y & z can be any real number within (-∞, ∞)
    Example 1
  • Indicate a few points that satisfy the equations x2 + y2 = 4 && z = 3
      • Points that would satisfy these 2 equations must first satisfy the 1st equation with x & y. Then, as long as the value of z remains 3, the point would be correct. Some points include:
    • (-2, 0, 3)
    • (2, 0, 3)
    • (0, -2, 3)
    • (0, 2, 3)
      •
      These 2 equations happen to form a circle located within the z+ axis where z = 3
    Measuring Distance between 2 points on a 3D Graph
  • The distance between 2 points P & Q (written as |PQ|)is given by the equation:
      • |PQ| = √((x - x0)2 + (y - y0)2 + (z - z0)2)

  • Squaring both sides of the equation leds to the Standard Equation for a Sphere as shown:
      • r2 = (x - x0)2 + (y - y0)2 + (z - z0)2 where r = radius
    Examples 2 & 3
  • Find the distance between P(2, 1, 5) & Q(-2, 3, 0)
      • The distance formula for points in space must be used. The distance will be solved as follows:
    • √(((-2) - (2))2 + ((3) - (1))2+ ((0) - (5))2)
    • √((-4)2 + (2)2 + (-5)2)
    • √(16 + 4 + 25)
    • √(45) = 3√(5)

  • Find the center (i, j, k) & radius r for the equation of a sphere given as x2 + y2 + z2 + 3x - 4z + 1 = 0
      • This can be done by finding the perfect square of the x, y and z-variables individually
    • (x + (3/2))2 - (3/2)2 + (y + 0)2 + (z - 2)2 - 4 = -1
    • (x + (3/2))2 + y2 + (z - 2)2 = 3 + (3/2)2
    • (x + (3/2))2 + y2 + (z - 2)2 = 21/4
    • Therefore, the center of the sphere = (-3/2, 0, 2) and the radius = √(21)/2
