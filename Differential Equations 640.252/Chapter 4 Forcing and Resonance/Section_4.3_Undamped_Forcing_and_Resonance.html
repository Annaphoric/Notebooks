Section 4.3: Undamping Forcing & Resonance

    Resonance
  • The equation for an undamped harmonic oscillator with sinusoidal forcing has the form:
      • $\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + g(y) y = f(y)$, where $f(y)$ is the sinusoidal forcing equation like $f(y) = sin(ω t)$ or $f(y) = cos(ω t)$

  • Resonance is the phenomenon that occurs when the frequency of the forcing equation approaches the natural frequency of the harmonic oscillator equation
      • The forcing frequency of the external force is always $\frac{ω}{2π}$
      The natural frequency is the frequency of the associated homogenous DE
      The external force is considered a resonant force when the harmonic oscillator is in exact resonance
    • At this point, the solutions grow towards increasing infinity in a linear fashion

  • The solution to a forced undamped harmonic oscillator has sinusoidal waves with amplitudes that increase & decrease
      • Beating occurs when the natural frequency & forced frequency are approximately the same
      In other words, an external force that is sinusoidal would cause the harmonic oscillator to beat when the frequencies of both are approximately the same
      The sinusoidal waves of the solutions create a sinusoidal pattern itself (waves make up a larger wave that beats)
    Example 1
      Find the general solution of the undamped equation $\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 2y = cos(ω t)$:

    1. Solving for the general solution of the associated homogenous unforced equation:
        2. $λ^2 + 2 = 0$
        $λ = i\sqrt{2}$

        $y_h(t) = k_1 cos(\sqrt{2}t) + k_2 sin(\sqrt{2}t)$

    2. Solving for a specific solution to the given DE:
        3. let $y_c(t) = α e^{ω i t}$
        $\frac{dy_c}{dt} = α ω i e^{ω i t}$
        $\frac{d^2y_c}{dt^2} = -α ω^2 e^{ω i t}$

        $-(α ω^2 e^{ω i t}) + 2α e^{ω i t} = e^{ω i t}$
        $α(2 - ω^2) = 1$
        $α = \frac{1}{2 - ω^2}$

        $y_c(t) = \frac{1}{2 - ω^2} e^{ω i t}$
        $y_c(t) = \frac{1}{2 - ω^2} (cos(ω t) + i sin(ω t))$

    3. General solution to the given DE:
        4. $y(t) = k_1 cos(\sqrt{2}t) + k_2 sin(\sqrt{2}t) + \frac{1}{2 - ω^2} cos(ω t)$
